COMPREHENSIVE REPARATIONS PLAN FOR THE 1921 TULSA RACE MASSACRE

Team Analysis and Strategic Approach (March 11, 2026)

---

EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

The 1921 Tulsa Race Massacre demands a multi-faceted, sustained approach that addresses historical truth, systemic inequality, and intergenerational harm. A single approach (legal, legislative, or monetary) will fail. Success requires simultaneous action across five channels:

1. Historical Correction & Truth-Telling
2. Systemic Wealth Transfer (not just individual payments)
3. Community-Led Justice (partnership model, not charity)
4. Legislation (state + federal)
5. Business Engagement (restitution from beneficiaries)

This plan acknowledges that:
- Survivors will likely not receive direct state compensation (OK Supreme Court rejected this)
- Descendants inherit documented disadvantage from the massacre
- The harm persists across 105+ years of systemic exclusion
- Justice delayed is inadequate, but better than no justice

---

PART 1: HISTORICAL CORRECTION (Foundation)

Goal: Establish irrefutable historical record and accountability

ACTIONS

1.1 Federal Historical Commission
- Expand on 2025 DOJ report with formal Congressional record
- Establish National Park Service historic site (Greenwood District)
- Require Oklahoma history curriculum to teach accurate massacre narrative
- Create documentary archive at Library of Congress
- Preserve survivor testimonies before all witnesses pass

Why: Without truth-telling, reparations lack moral foundation. Historical acknowledgment validates victims and prevents erasure.

1.2 Local Historical Preservation
- Support Justice for Greenwood Foundation's genealogical research
- Digitize 45,000+ released records
- Create interactive timeline and victim database
- Establish Greenwood Museum as permanent institution
- Annual June 1st Tulsa Race Massacre Remembrance Day

Why: Community ownership of narrative. Local institutions sustain memory better than federal mandates.

1.3 Educational Standards
- Update state history curriculum (currently incomplete)
- Integrate into AP U.S. History, ethnic studies, civics
- Create high school / college curriculum
- Requirement: Teach both white and Black perspectives, including accountability
- Teacher training on sensitive historical topics

Why: Next generation learns truth, prevents historical amnesia, normalizes accountability.

COST ESTIMATE: $15M-25M over 10 years
RESPONSIBILITY: Federal government (DOJ, NPS) + Oklahoma Department of Education
TIMELINE: 1-2 years to establish, 10+ years to execute

PART 2: SYSTEMIC WEALTH TRANSFER (Core)

Goal: Close wealth gaps created by the massacre

The massacre destroyed $100M in property (1921 dollars). Survivors lost homes, businesses, 100+ years of compounding wealth. Descendants inherit this deficit.

2.1 Direct Restitution for Survivors (Immediate)
- Remaining survivors (3 as of 2026): $500,000 each
- Total: ~$1.5M
- Justification: Direct victims of documented government violence
- Source: Federal government (Department of Justice accountability fund)
- Delivery: Direct payment, no conditions
- Timeline: ASAP (survivors are 111+ years old)

Why: Survivors may not live to see broader reparations. Direct payment is moral imperative.

2.2 Verified Descendant Compensation (Medium-term, 10 years)
- Identify descendants via genealogical research (230+ families already identified)
- Tiered payment based on documented harm:
  * Direct descendants of victims: $100,000 each
  * Greenwood property owners' heirs: $50,000 each
  * Community members displaced: $25,000 each
- Total: ~$50M-75M over 10 years
- Source: State trust fund + federal matching + private donors
- Delivery: Annual payments to prevent lump-sum consumption; required financial literacy coaching

Why: Systemic wealth transfer closes generational gap. Gradual payment encourages financial planning.

2.3 Community Wealth Building (10-25 year program)
Goal: Close racial wealth gap in Greenwood and North Tulsa

Programs:
- Homeownership down-payment assistance: $100M
  * First-time homebuyers in Greenwood/North Tulsa
  * Descendants prioritized but open to all Black Tulsans
  * 30-year mortgages with favorable terms
  
- Small business grants and loans: $50M
  * Descendants prioritized
  * Grants (not loans) for startups
  * Below-market-rate loans for expansion
  * Business coaching / mentoring
  
- Higher education endowment: $100M
  * Full scholarships for verified descendants
  * Preference for historically Black colleges
  * K-12 tutoring and college prep in Greenwood schools
  
- Healthcare infrastructure: $25M
  * New community health center in Greenwood
  * Addresses health disparities from generational stress
  * Trauma-informed mental health services
  
- Workforce development: $25M
  * Job training in high-demand fields
  * Apprenticeships with local employers
  * Priority hiring for Greenwood residents

Total: $300M+ over 25 years

Why: Wealth gaps don't close with one-time payments. Systemic programs build sustainable advantage.

COST ESTIMATE: $350M-450M over 25 years
RESPONSIBILITY: State of Oklahoma (75%) + Federal government (25%) + Private donors
TIMELINE: 1-2 years to structure, 25 years to execute
FUNDING SOURCES:
- State bonding authority
- Federal grant programs
- Private foundations (Justice for Greenwood, MacArthur, Gates, Ford)
- Perpetual endowment model (earnings fund programs indefinitely)

PART 3: LEGAL ACCOUNTABILITY (Parallel Track)

Goal: Establish legal precedent that governments can harm communities, even if criminal prosecution isn't possible

3.1 State-Level Accountability
- Lawsuit: State of Oklahoma + Tulsa Police Department for negligence/complicity
- Damages: Acknowledge government's role in massacre
- Claims: 
  * Failure to protect citizens from mob violence
  * Active participation by National Guard
  * Detention in internment camps
  * Seizure and destruction of property
- Target: $100M+ settlement
- Why State Court? Establishes state-level accountability, avoids federal complications
- Expected Outcome: Settlement + apology, not criminal charges

3.2 Federal Civil Rights
- DOJ enforcement under 18 U.S.C. § 249 (federal hate crime law)
- Problem: Statute of limitations expired (massacre in 1921, prosecution window closed in late 1920s)
- Alternative: Legislation extending statute of limitations for historical civil rights violations
- If successful: Federal prosecution of perpetrators (if any still living), federal reparations fund

3.3 Precedent-Setting Lawsuits
- Target other unresolved historical atrocities:
  * Rosewood, Florida (1923)
  * Elaine, Arkansas (1919)
  * Wilmington, North Carolina (1898)
- Collective action: National consortium of descendants, historians, civil rights organizations
- Goal: Federal reparations program for all racial atrocities (like Holocaust, Japanese internment)

COST ESTIMATE: $10M-20M in legal fees (funded by Justice for Greenwood, NAACP Legal Defense)
RESPONSIBILITY: State attorney general + private civil rights attorneys
TIMELINE: 2-3 years to trial, settlement within 5 years
EXPECTED OUTCOME: $100M+ state settlement + federal legislation

PART 4: LEGISLATION (Structural Change)

Goal: Make Tulsa a model for state-level reparations, create federal framework

4.1 Oklahoma State Legislation (Already in Progress)
- HB 1024 (2021): Reparations study commission (completed)
- Next: Pass comprehensive reparations bill
- Components:
  * Establish perpetual trust fund ($500M+)
  * Create descendant registry and verification process
  * Fund all wealth-building programs (homeownership, business, education)
  * Recognize June 1st as official state holiday
  * Require history curriculum update
  * Tax incentives for businesses investing in Greenwood

4.2 Federal Reparations Legislation
- Build on Tulsa model
- H.R. 40 framework (40-acre-and-a-mule equivalent for modern era)
- Components:
  * Federal Reparations Commission (like Truth and Reconciliation)
  * Establish reparations for multiple historical atrocities (Tulsa, Rosewood, Elaine, etc.)
  * Create federal matching funds for state reparations programs
  * Protect BIPOC communities from future atrocities
  * Fund permanent institutional memory (museums, education, research)

- Why federal? State alone can't generate $500M+ sustainably. Federal backing legitimizes broader movement.

4.3 Legislation Timeline
- Year 1: Pass Oklahoma reparations bill (uses Greenwood Trust as template)
- Years 2-3: Federal legislation (H.R. 40 advance)
- Years 3-10: Implementation and funding
- Ongoing: Protect from political backlash, maintain funding through economic cycles

COST ESTIMATE: $500M+ in legislation + $1B+ in implementation
RESPONSIBILITY: Oklahoma legislature + U.S. Congress
TIMELINE: 3-5 years for passage, 25+ years for full execution

PART 5: BUSINESS ENGAGEMENT (Restitution Model)

Goal: Identify companies that benefited from the massacre, require restitution

5.1 Beneficiary Companies
Research question: Which businesses were built on Greenwood's destruction?

Examples:
- Banks that foreclosed on Black property after massacre
- Real estate companies that bought destroyed property at pennies on dollar
- Insurance companies that refused to pay claims to Black victims
- Oil companies that extracted wealth from Greenwood land
- Construction firms hired to rebuild (enriched by destruction)
- Historical monopolies (utilities, retail) that operated under segregation

Actions:
- Audit company origins (1921-1950)
- Document profits from Greenwood displacement
- Calculate restitution (original profit + 105 years compound interest)
- Approach companies with historical responsibility
- Create "Corporate Reparations" commitment (public relations + moral imperative)

5.2 Corporate Commitments
- Target major Tulsa-based and national companies
- Donations to Greenwood Trust: $500K-$5M per company
- Scholarship funds: $1M+ per major corporation
- Workforce investment: Hiring/mentoring programs for Greenwood residents
- Why? Companies benefit from public trust, historical redemption improves brand

5.3 Investor Pressure
- Use ESG (Environmental, Social, Governance) frameworks
- Require public companies to acknowledge historical harm
- Investor activism: Pension funds, foundations, universities demand reparations commitments
- Model: Like tobacco reparations, Holocaust restitution from German companies

COST ESTIMATE: $100M-500M (voluntary from corporations + investor pressure)
RESPONSIBILITY: Justice for Greenwood Foundation + descendant organizations + investors
TIMELINE: Ongoing, escalating pressure

PART 6: COMMUNITY-LED JUSTICE (Ownership Model)

Goal: Ensure Black Tulsans and descendants drive reparations, not white institutions

6.1 Descendant-Led Governance
- Reparations decisions made BY Black descendants, not FOR them
- Board structure: 70% descendants, 20% historians, 10% government
- Community veto power: Descendants can reject proposals
- Transparency: Public meetings, regular reporting, financial accountability

6.2 Justice for Greenwood Foundation (Expanded Role)
- Transition from advocacy to governance
- Become primary steward of reparations funds
- Direct all community-level decisions
- Hire Black staff, economists, historians
- Partner with historically Black colleges for research

6.3 Truth and Reconciliation (Optional)
- Survivors/descendants share stories (not required)
- Perpetrators' descendants acknowledge harm (not punishment)
- Community healing circles (not legal proceedings)
- Model: South African Truth and Reconciliation, but modified for U.S. context
- Goal: Collective acknowledgment, not individual blame

6.4 Intergenerational Justice
- Children born during reparations program benefit
- Create pathway for second, third generation advantage
- 50-year program horizon (not 10 years)
- Evaluate progress via wealth gap closure, not just payment amounts

COST ESTIMATE: $50M+ in organizational capacity (research, governance, administration)
RESPONSIBILITY: Justice for Greenwood Foundation + Black descendants
TIMELINE: Ongoing, sustained over 50 years

WHY THIS PLAN WORKS

1. ACKNOWLEDGES LEGAL LIMITS
- Courts won't grant reparations (OK Supreme Court already ruled)
- Criminal prosecution impossible (statute of limitations)
- BUT: Political, legislative, business channels still available
- Doesn't wait for perfect legal remedy; pursues imperfect justice immediately

2. ADDRESSES ROOT CAUSE (Wealth Inequality)
- One-time payments don't close 105-year wealth gap
- Systemic programs build sustained advantage
- Homeownership, education, business ownership compound over generations
- Targets the mechanism of harm (economic exclusion), not just symptoms

3. BUILDS COMMUNITY AGENCY
- Descendants lead decisions, not charity organizations
- Ownership breeds sustainability
- Community members become stewards, not passive recipients
- Creates political constituency for long-term funding

4. CREATES ACCOUNTABILITY WITHOUT PUNISHMENT
- Companies acknowledge harm, pay restitution
- Government apologizes, provides funds
- Individuals aren't prosecuted (many deceased anyway)
- Focus: Forward-looking (fix the future), not backward-looking (punish the past)

5. SETS NATIONAL PRECEDENT
- Tulsa becomes model for Rosewood, Elaine, and other atrocities
- Federal legislation legitimizes reparations nationwide
- Changes national conversation: "If Tulsa, why not slavery?"
- Creates framework for restorative justice in America

6. SUSTAINABLE OVER TIME
- Endowment model funds programs indefinitely
- Political cycles won't defund via tax cuts (corpus protected)
- Measurable outcomes keep program on track
- 50-year horizon allows intergenerational wealth-building

ARGUMENTS FOR EACH APPROACH

Historical Correction
"We cannot repair what we deny. Truth-telling is the foundation of justice."
- Without acknowledging the massacre clearly, reparations feel like handouts
- International precedent: Holocaust education, Japanese internment museums
- Prevents repeat atrocities: A nation that forgets its sins repeats them

Systemic Wealth Transfer
"One-time payments won't close a 105-year wealth gap. Generational advantage requires generational investment."
- Wealth compounds: $100K in 1921 is $3.5M in 2026
- Descendants inherit disadvantage as surely as white Americans inherit advantage
- Homeownership, education, business creation are the mechanisms of wealth
- Target those mechanisms

Legal Accountability
"Governments must face consequences for harming citizens, even if criminal law can't reach them."
- State lawsuits establish precedent: Governments are accountable
- Civil litigation produces damages without criminal stigma
- International model: Holocaust restitution from governments + companies
- Creates insurance against future atrocities (if reputationally costly, governments prevent them)

Legislation
"Permanent programs require permanent law. Funding must survive political cycles."
- Discretionary appropriations get cut; legislated funding endures
- State law protects from federal defunding
- Federal law prevents other states from backsliding
- Moral legitimacy: Nation says "this matters" in law, not just words

Business Engagement
"Companies that profited from injustice should participate in remedy. ESG pressure makes this inevitable."
- Voluntary compliance better than forced
- Brand benefit encourages large donations
- International model: Holocaust restitution from German, Swiss companies
- Creates business constituency for reparations (unlikely but possible)

Community Leadership
"Reparations designed by white institutions will fail. Descendants must lead."
- Historical parallel: Native Americans forced into white-designed programs (failed)
- Psychological benefit: Ownership, dignity, agency
- Practical benefit: Better solutions, higher community buy-in
- Moral requirement: Victims determine remedy, not perpetrators

COUNTER-ARGUMENTS & RESPONSES

"This is too expensive ($500M+)"
Response: Consider the cost of 105 years of systemic exclusion. Also compare:
- Iraq War: $2 trillion
- 2008 bank bailouts: $700 billion
- COVID stimulus: $5 trillion
- Tulsa reparations: $500 million = 0.00025% of federal budget
- Also: Cost paid by state, federal, and private (corporations) sources, not all on taxpayers

"Why should we pay for something that happened 105 years ago?"
Response: Because:
- The harm persists: Wealth gaps compound over generations
- Precedent: Holocaust restitution (70+ years later), Japanese internment (50+ years later)
- Practical: Addressing systemic inequality improves public health, crime, economy
- Moral: America regularly pays military pensions for wars 100+ years old

"Should we also do reparations for slavery? This opens a floodgate."
Response: Yes, this SHOULD open a floodgate. Tulsa is a smaller, more recent, more documentable case. Success here creates model for slavery reparations. But also:
- Tulsa is more solvable (specific incident, identified victims, living survivors)
- Each injustice requires tailored approach
- Other communities will demand justice (Rosewood, Elaine)
- National reparations program is inevitable; Tulsa accelerates it

"Descendants aren't responsible for actions of ancestors."
Response: True. But descendants DID inherit the consequences:
- Wealth gaps (measurable)
- Health disparities (measurable)
- Educational gaps (measurable)
- These are documented harm, not "descendant responsibility"
- Reparations address the HARM, not punish the person

ULTIMATE GOAL

By 2050, North Tulsa and Greenwood District achieve economic parity with broader Tulsa:
- Median household income: Equal to city average
- Homeownership rate: Equal to city average
- College completion: Equal to city average
- Business creation: Equal to city average
- Life expectancy: Equal to city average
- Health outcomes: Equal to city average

This isn't "special treatment." It's remedying documented harm.

Success means: An outside observer looking at Tulsa economic data in 2050 cannot identify which neighborhoods were Black, which were white, which suffered the massacre.

That's the goal. That's justice.
