THE TULSA RACE MASSACRE: HISTORY, AFTERMATH, AND REPARATIONS
================================================================================

PART 1: GREENWOOD BEFORE THE MASSACRE - "BLACK WALL STREET"

In 1921, the Greenwood District of Tulsa, Oklahoma was the most prosperous Black 
community in America. Known as "Black Wall Street," Greenwood thrived with:

• Over 35 blocks of Black-owned businesses
• Restaurants, theaters, hotels, and professional offices
• Two daily newspapers in multiple languages
• A population of approximately 10,000 people
• Estimated wealth of $1 million (roughly $17 million today)

Greenwood's success came from a diverse economy: oil derricks on nearby land, 
transportation hubs, entrepreneurship, and a self-sufficient community that kept 
wealth circulating internally. Black professionals served the Black community; 
Black merchants sold to Black customers.


PART 2: THE MASSACRE - MAY 31 - JUNE 1, 1921

Timeline of Events:

MAY 30, 1921: Sarah Page (a 17-year-old white girl) and Dick Rowland (a 19-year-old 
Black boy) encounter in an elevator at a downtown building. The details remain unclear, 
but Page screams. Rowland flees.

MAY 31, AFTERNOON: Local newspapers print inflammatory articles. Police arrest Rowland. 
A mob of white residents gathers at the jail.

MAY 31, EVENING: Black leaders from Greenwood arrive at the jail to prevent a lynching. 
A confrontation occurs. Gunfire erupts.

JUNE 1, OVERNIGHT: White mobs invade Greenwood. They burn buildings, loot businesses, 
and kill residents indiscriminately. Black residents flee in panic. The violence lasted 
approximately 18 hours.

CASUALTIES AND DESTRUCTION:

• Death toll: Official count was 36 (believed to be much higher—estimates range 100-300+)
• Businesses destroyed: 1,256 homes, 191 businesses, churches, schools
• Arrests: Over 6,000 Black residents detained in detention camps
• Economic loss: Estimated $30 million in property (roughly $500 million today)
• Displacement: Most of Greenwood's population scattered; many fled Oklahoma entirely


PART 3: THE AFTERMATH AND 80-YEAR SILENCE

What happened after the massacre is remarkable: silence. For nearly 80 years, the Tulsa 
Race Massacre was omitted from history books, not taught in schools, and rarely discussed 
publicly. In Oklahoma, it was erased from official narratives.

Why the silence?

• Civic shame: City leaders wanted to move forward and avoid the stigma
• Media suppression: Local newspapers were destroyed or silent
• Systemic erasure: The story did not fit the preferred narrative of American race relations
• Intergenerational trauma: Survivors and their families were traumatized and dispersed

The silence began to break in the 1990s when historians and journalists rediscovered 
the story. In 2001, the Oklahoma Historical Society released the official account. 
In 2020, the centennial sparked renewed national attention.


PART 4: REPARATIONS - THE DEBATE

What are reparations?

Reparations are compensation (typically financial) to descendants of enslaved or harmed 
people for historical wrongs and ongoing systemic inequalities. In the Tulsa context, 
they would address the destruction of wealth, property, and lives.

ARGUMENTS FOR REPARATIONS:

1. Legal precedent: The U.S. has paid reparations to Japanese Americans (WWII internment) 
   and Native Americans

2. Documented harm: The massacre destroyed approximately $30 million in property and wealth

3. Intergenerational impact: Survivors' descendants were denied inherited wealth, 
   education, and opportunities

4. Healing and justice: Reparations symbolize acknowledgment, accountability, and a path 
   toward reconciliation

5. Specificity: The Tulsa massacre is well-documented; victims and descendants are 
   identifiable

ARGUMENTS AGAINST REPARATIONS:

1. Cost concerns: Reparations could be financially burdensome for the state or city

2. Liability questions: Who exactly should pay? The city? The state? The descendants 
   of perpetrators?

3. Implementation challenges: How to identify eligible recipients and determine fair amounts?

4. Moving forward: Some argue that focusing on the past hinders progress and unity

5. Alternative solutions: Some prefer education, memorials, or economic development 
   programs instead of direct payments

The debate remains active. Some propose direct monetary reparations, while others advocate 
for community investment, education programs, or memorial initiatives.


PART 5: HUMAN STORIES - SURVIVORS AND DESCENDANTS

The massacre wasn't just statistics—it affected real families:

Olivia Hooker (1912-2018): One of the youngest survivors. She was 6 years old when her 
home burned. She lived 106 years, became a child psychologist, and spent her later years 
testifying about the massacre and advocating for education about it.

J.B. Jackson (1913-1997): A successful businessman and survivor. He rebuilt his life but 
never forgot the violence. His descendants today carry both pride in his resilience and 
grief over what was lost.

Dr. A.J. Smitherman (founder of the Tulsa Star newspaper): Though he survived, he was 
arrested and driven out of Oklahoma. His publishing legacy and community leadership were 
destroyed.

Modern descendants: Today's Greenwood community members and descendants of massacre 
survivors carry a complex legacy—pride in ancestral achievement, grief over loss, and 
ongoing questions about justice.
